# Gold Standard Reference — Nail Salon Cold Outreach
# Retro Nail Bar / Tucson, AZ

This is the reference script that established the realism standard for Precision Workz outreach.
Study the thinking pattern, not the exact words. Apply the same level of specific, honest,
industry-aware, proof-first thinking to every other business type.

---

## What made this script work

1. Every claim was verifiable in under 10 seconds on any phone.
2. It led with a genuine positive observation before naming the gap.
3. The gap was framed as friction to close, not proof the business was failing.
4. The first ask was screenshots — not a meeting, not a pitch, not a demo.
5. It acknowledged the business's actual strengths (4.7 stars, 42 reviews) instead of pretending they didn't exist.
6. It matched the specific issues to what an appointment-only nail salon actually needs.

---

## The recommended opener (verbatim reference)

> "Hey, this is [Name] with Precision Workz here in Tucson. I looked at Retro Nail Bar's Google profile before calling. The reviews are solid — that part is working.
>
> The main thing I noticed is that for an appointment-only salon, there's no booking button showing on the profile. And the first photos are more logo and exterior than nail work. Both are fixable.
>
> Would it be okay if I texted you a couple of screenshots showing exactly what I mean?"

---

## Observations that made the script specific

- Performance score 67 on Lighthouse — flagged as needs improvement
- Largest Contentful Paint 10.4 seconds — well above the 2.5s target
- No booking button in the GBP action bar
- First hero photos: logo and office building exterior, not nail work
- 1-star review visible on profile with no owner response
- Attributes section shows only "Accepts credit cards" and "Restroom"
- 42 reviews — potentially fewer than top Tucson competitors
- Services section sparse or not visible in public search view

---

## Why the screenshot-first CTA worked here

Asking "Would it be okay if I texted you screenshots?" works because:
- It requires almost no commitment from the owner
- It proves the call was researched, not generic
- It gives the owner evidence to look at on their own time
- Once they look at the screenshots, the conversation has already started
- It is a smaller yes than scheduling a call

The audit call should come after they engage with the screenshots, not before.

## Banned lines that were removed (and why)

| Line | Why Banned |
|---|---|
| "You're losing customers every day." | Implies confirmed lost revenue with no data |
| "I can get you top 3 on Google Maps." | Ranking guarantee — never defensible |
| "Google is punishing you." | False framing, no evidence |
| "Your profile is broken." | Overstated and insulting |
| "You're invisible online." | False — 4.7 stars and 42 reviews |
| "Your reviews are better than most." | Unverified comparison |
| "The top 3 get X% of clicks." | Unsourced statistic |
| "This will pay for itself." | Implied revenue guarantee |
| "No pitch, just the audit." | This is a pitch |
| "I helped a client do exactly this." | Requires a real documented case study |

## Transferable thinking pattern

When approaching any business:

1. Spend 5-10 minutes on their Google profile, website, and Lighthouse before calling.
2. Find one thing that is genuinely working. Note it honestly.
3. Find the most specific, most visible, most fixable gap.
4. Build the opener around that one gap.
5. Make the first ask screenshots, not a meeting.
6. Frame every issue as friction worth closing, not a failing business.
7. Remove anything a skeptical owner could not verify in 30 seconds on their phone.

The goal is not to sound like this nail salon script.
The goal is to use the same level of specific, honest, industry-aware thinking for every business.
